## 1. The Big Picture: U.S. Equity Markets, 2015–2024

Over the last decade, U.S. equities delivered well-above-average returns despite two sharp drawdowns (the COVID crash in early 2020 and the 2022 bear market driven by aggressive Federal Reserve rate hikes). The S&P 500's annualized total return for 2015–2024 was roughly **13% per year**, comfortably above its long-run ~10% average since 1928 (Moomoo summary of S&P 500 historical performance, June 2025; NYU Stern *Historical Returns on Stocks, Bonds and Bills: 1928–2024*).

## 2. Decade in Context: What Drove Markets

**2015–2016 — Volatile sideways:** Concerns about a China growth slowdown, an oil-price crash, and the Brexit referendum (June 2016) drove choppy markets. Returns were modest.

**2017 — "Synchronized global growth":** Low volatility, broad rally; the S&P 500 didn't have a single drawdown larger than 3% all year.

**2018 — Rate-hike scare and tariff fears:** The Federal Reserve raised rates four times; Q4 saw a sharp sell-off, leaving all three major indexes red on the year.

**2019 — Recovery rally:** The Fed pivoted dovish; equities snapped back hard, with the S&P 500 posting its best year since 2013.

**2020 — COVID crash and unprecedented recovery:** The S&P 500 fell ~34% from February to March 23, then rallied to finish positive thanks to massive fiscal and monetary stimulus. Tech and "stay-at-home" names led — the Nasdaq surged +43.6%.

**2021 — Liquidity boom:** Stimulus and zero-rate policy fueled record highs across indexes; meme-stock and SPAC manias also peaked.

**2022 — Worst year since 2008:** The Fed raised rates from ~0% to 4.25–4.5% to fight 40-year-high inflation. The Nasdaq lost roughly a third of its value, and bonds also fell — an unusually bad year for diversified portfolios.

**2023–2024 — The AI rally:** Generative AI enthusiasm (kicked off by ChatGPT's November 2022 launch) drove enormous gains in semiconductor and platform stocks. NVIDIA's market cap multiplied several times over; the so-called "Magnificent Seven" mega-cap tech names accounted for an outsized share of index gains.

### 2.1 Best- and Worst-Performing S&P 500 Sectors, 2015–2024

According to Ben Carlson's *A Wealth of Common Sense* "2024 Sector Quilt" analysis (January 2025):

- **Best sector:** Technology, **~+20% annualized** over the decade.
- **Worst sector:** Energy, **~+5% annualized** — a striking reversal from 2000–2014, when Energy was the best-performing sector.
- Only a handful of sectors managed double-digit 10-year annual returns; Technology dominated by a wide margin.

## 3. Politicians and Stock Trading

Members of the U.S. Congress can legally trade individual stocks, subject to the **STOCK Act (2012)**, which requires disclosure of trades over $1,000 within 45 days via Periodic Transaction Reports (PTRs). Penalties for late disclosure are typically only a $200 fine per violation, and broad dollar ranges in disclosures make exact returns hard to verify.

Reporting from *Unusual Whales*, *Fortune*, *The Hill*, and *Common Cause* consistently finds that **a meaningful subset of Congress outperforms the S&P 500 in any given year**, though the overall distribution of Congressional returns roughly tracks the market average.

> Important caveat: estimated returns below are based on PTR disclosures, mid-range dollar estimates, and end-of-year prices. They are approximations — not audited returns. They also do not constitute proof of insider trading; many of these names sit on committees whose oversight overlaps with their holdings, which is a *conflict-of-interest* concern, not a legal conclusion.

### 3.1 Unusual Whales 2024 Congressional Trading Report — Headline Findings

Per the [Unusual Whales 2024 report](https://unusualwhales.com/congress-trading-report-2024) (published January 7, 2025) and follow-on coverage by *Fortune* and *The Hill*:

- The average **Democrat-traded portfolio gained ~+31%** in 2024.
- The average **Republican-traded portfolio gained ~+26%**.
- The S&P 500 (SPY) returned roughly **+25%** the same year.
- Of the ~100 actively trading members analyzed, roughly half beat the S&P 500.
- Top holdings concentrated in **technology (semiconductors, software), financials, and energy**.
- The Democrat-tracking **NANC ETF** returned ~+27% in 2024; the Republican-tracking **KRUZ ETF** returned ~+14%.

### 3.3 Notable Pre-Decade Cases Still in the Public Record

- **David Perdue (R-GA, former Senator):** Subject of a DOJ inquiry over early-2020 trades, including repeated PFE purchases shortly before Pfizer announced its COVID-19 vaccine partnership with BioNTech. He was not charged.
- **Kelly Loeffler (R-GA, former Senator):** Investigated for sales of up to $4.6M in stocks between a January 24, 2020 closed-door COVID briefing and the March 2020 market crash. She was not charged.
- **Richard Burr (R-NC, former Senator):** Investigated over similar early-2020 sales; DOJ closed the case without charges in 2021.

### 3.4 Why the Topic Matters

- Members of Congress vote on legislation, sit on oversight committees, and receive non-public briefings (e.g., classified intelligence, regulatory drafts) that retail investors do not have access to.
- *Common Cause* reported in December 2025 that Congress made over **$635 million in stock trades** during the 119th Congress's first session, with bipartisan calls for reform continuing.
- Despite at least **10 bipartisan bills** introduced from 2021–2024 to ban Congressional stock trading (e.g., the **ETHICS Act**, **PELOSI Act**, **TRUST in Congress Act**, **Bipartisan Restoring Faith in Government Act**), none reached a floor vote as of *Unusual Whales*' January 2025 report.

## 4. Key Takeaways

1. **Long-run U.S. equity returns over 2015–2024 were exceptional** (~13% annualized for the S&P 500), driven largely by mega-cap technology and a powerful post-2022 AI rally.
2. **Drawdowns were sharp but short.** COVID (2020) and the 2022 rate-hike bear market were both followed by strong rebounds within roughly 12–18 months.
3. **Sector concentration mattered enormously.** Tech delivered ~20%/year for the decade; Energy delivered ~5%. Passive S&P 500 investors benefited from cap-weighting that ramped exposure to winning sectors automatically.
4. **A subset of Congress reliably outperforms the market**, especially active traders concentrated in mega-cap tech. Whether this reflects skill, structural information advantages, or both remains the subject of ongoing reform debates.
5. **Disclosure ≠ regulation.** The STOCK Act provides transparency but minimal enforcement; multiple bipartisan reform bills have stalled.

## 2. Year-by-Year Macro Narrative

### 2015 — Volatile Sideways

A mixed year dominated by external shocks. China's SSE Composite fell roughly 43% between June and August 2015, the Greek debt default in June, and the U.S. Federal Reserve's eventual December 2015 rate-hike-from-zero (the first since 2006) drove sharp volatility. The S&P 500 finished essentially flat (+1.38% total return); the Dow ended down 2.23%, its first negative year since 2008 (Wikipedia, "2015–2016 stock market selloff"; Bright Directions *2015 Stock Market in Review*).

### 2016 — Worst Start in History, Strong Finish

The S&P 500's worst-ever start to a year (−8% by mid-January, on crude oil collapsing below $27/barrel) gave way to a recovery rally. The Brexit referendum on June 23 triggered the largest single-day global market loss in history at the time — an estimated **$2 trillion** wiped out in 24 June 2016 trading (CNBC). Markets recovered within days, and the year ended solidly positive.

### 2017 — Extraordinary Calm

A textbook low-volatility melt-up. The S&P 500 did not have a single drawdown deeper than ~3% all year. Synchronized global growth, low rates, and December's Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (which sharply reduced the U.S. corporate tax rate) all contributed.

### 2018 — Rate-Hike Scare and Trade War

The Fed raised rates four times. Combined with Trump-administration tariffs on China, Q4 saw a near-bear-market sell-off; the S&P 500 fell roughly 19.8% peak-to-trough between September and December 24. All three major U.S. indexes finished negative for the year.

### 2019 — Dovish Pivot Rally

The Fed reversed course, cutting rates three times. Stocks snapped back hard — the S&P 500 returned +31.5% (total), its best calendar year since 2013.

### 2020 — Fastest Bear Market and Fastest Recovery in History

After peaking on February 19, 2020, the S&P 500 fell **34% in just 33 days**, bottoming on March 23 — the fastest bear market on record (BNY, "Crisis and Comeback"). The Federal Reserve cut rates to zero and launched unlimited quantitative easing; Congress passed the $2.2 trillion CARES Act. The S&P 500 reclaimed its prior all-time high by **August 18, 2020** — roughly five months from low to recovery (NPR, August 2020). The Nasdaq surged +43.6%, led by "stay-at-home" beneficiaries like Amazon, Zoom, and Netflix.

### 2021 — Liquidity Boom

Zero-rate policy, additional stimulus, and continued QE drove the S&P 500 to **70 record closing highs** during the year. SPAC mergers and meme-stock manias (GameStop, AMC) peaked. Crypto also surged before reversing late in the year.

### 2022 — Worst Year Since 2008

Inflation hit a 40-year high (CPI peaked at 9.1% in June 2022). The Federal Reserve raised the fed funds rate from ~0% to **4.25–4.50% in nine months** — including four consecutive 75-basis-point hikes from June through November (Forbes, *Federal Funds Rate History*). The S&P 500 peaked at 4,796 on January 3, 2022, and fell roughly **25% to its October 12 low** (Wikipedia, *2022 stock market decline*). Bonds also fell sharply — the worst year for a traditional 60/40 portfolio since the Great Depression.

### 2023 — The AI Rally Begins

ChatGPT's late-2022 launch sparked sustained generative-AI enthusiasm. NVIDIA stock more than tripled (+239% price). The "Magnificent Seven" (Apple, Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, NVIDIA, Tesla) collectively returned **+76%**, versus +24% for the broader S&P 500, and accounted for roughly **63% of the index's total gain** (Mellon Investments, "A Closer Look at Magnificent Seven Stocks"; Landmark Wealth Management).

### 2024 — AI Rally Broadens, Slightly

The S&P 500 hit **57 record closes**. The Magnificent Seven returned approximately **+48% on average** vs. +25% for the index, contributing an estimated **~55–60%** of the index's gain (Yahoo Finance / Vantage Markets). Fed rate cuts began in September 2024 (a 50-bp cut), helping smaller stocks rally late in the year.

## 4. The "Magnificent Seven" Era

The term "Magnificent Seven" — coined by Bank of America's Michael Hartnett in 2023 — refers to seven U.S. mega-cap technology stocks: **Apple, Microsoft, Alphabet (Google), Amazon, Meta, NVIDIA, and Tesla**.

| Year | Mag 7 Avg. Return | S&P 500 Return | Mag 7 Share of S&P 500 Gain |
|------|-------------------|----------------|------------------------------|
| 2023 | +75.7% | +24.2% | ~63% |
| 2024 | +47.6% | +23.3% | ~55–60% |

*Sources: Mellon Investments; Landmark Wealth Management; Vantage Markets; Yahoo Finance.*

By the end of 2024, the Magnificent Seven combined accounted for roughly **30% of the S&P 500's total market capitalization**, a historic level of concentration. This concentration is one reason that "passive" S&P 500 investing during 2023–2024 was, functionally, a heavy bet on AI-driven mega-cap tech.

NVIDIA in particular went from a roughly $400 billion market cap in late 2022 to **over $3 trillion** by mid-2024 — briefly the largest company in the world.

## 5. Politicians and Stock Trading — Overview

The 2012 **STOCK Act** ("Stop Trading on Congressional Knowledge Act") requires members of the U.S. Congress, their spouses, and dependent children to disclose stock, bond, and certain other security trades over **$1,000 within 45 days** via Periodic Transaction Reports (PTRs). The standard penalty for late filing is a **$200 fine**, which the House and Senate ethics committees may waive (Business Insider, Dec 2021; NPR, July 2024).

### 5.1 Aggregate Performance — Unusual Whales 2024 Report

The Unusual Whales 2024 Congressional Trading Report (published January 7, 2025) found, based on estimated portfolio changes from PTR disclosures:

- **Democrat-traded portfolios:** +31% average estimated return in 2024
- **Republican-traded portfolios:** +26% average estimated return
- **S&P 500 (SPY) benchmark:** ~+25%
- **Total assets traded:** ~$564 million (≈$524M individual stocks + $40M ETFs/funds)
- **Top sectors held:** Technology ($159M), Financial Services ($72M), Consumer Cyclical ($59M), Industrials ($51M), Energy ($44M)

About half of actively trading members beat the S&P 500; the rest underperformed or roughly tracked it. The Democratic advantage in 2024 was driven primarily by greater allocation to big tech (NVDA, AMZN, META, GOOGL), while Republican-leaning portfolios concentrated more in financials, energy, and commodities (Unusual Whales, *Congress Trading Report 2024*).

### 5.2 Important Caveats

- Disclosed dollar amounts are in **wide ranges** (e.g., "$1,001–$15,000"); estimated returns rely on midpoint approximations.
- "Outperformance" in any single year does not prove insider trading or skill. Random variation alone produces a meaningful tail of outperformers each year.
- Many holdings are managed by trustees or financial advisers, with varying degrees of member involvement.
- The relationship between committee assignments and holdings is a **conflict-of-interest** concern, not a criminal-law conclusion. No U.S. member of Congress has been criminally convicted for trading on insider information since the STOCK Act's passage.

## 6. Politician Deep Dives

The members below are described in publicly available reporting as notable traders. Inclusion here is **descriptive of news coverage and disclosure data**, not an endorsement, accusation, or recommendation.

### 6.1 Nancy Pelosi (D-CA, House)

Position: U.S. Representative since 1987; Speaker of the House 2007–2011, 2019–2023. Currently a senior member.

**Reported 2024 portfolio return:** approximately **+54%** based on PTR-disclosed holdings (Investing.com, January 2025; secondary reporting via Capitol Trades).

**Trading style:** Disclosures show concentrated, often large bets in mega-cap technology, typically via **deep-in-the-money LEAPs (long-dated call options)** taken out after notable drawdowns. Actual trades are executed by her husband, **Paul Pelosi**, a longtime venture capitalist and investor. They have no formal blind trust.

**Notable 2023–2024 disclosed trades:**
- **NVIDIA (NVDA)** — long calls; Unusual Whales reported the position was up ~273% by end of 2024.
- **Palo Alto Networks (PANW)** — long calls; reported up ~93%.
- **Broadcom (AVGO)** — long calls; reported up ~97%.
- **Tempus AI (TEM)** — purchased 50 call options pre-IPO; exercised in January 2026 into 5,000 shares (U.S. News Money; Capitol Trades, January 2025).
- **Databricks** — disclosed private investment exceeding $1 million in 2024.
- **December 2024 sales** of large blocks of AAPL, NVDA, AMZN, DIS, PYPL (multiple disclosures; Earn Your Leisure / Instagram coverage of January 2025 filings).

**Conflict-of-interest context:** As former Speaker and a senior House Democrat, Pelosi influences legislation broadly. The 2022 CHIPS and Science Act, which directed roughly $52 billion to U.S. semiconductor manufacturing, was passed during a period when her family held NVIDIA call options.

**Investigation status:** None. Pelosi has stated she does not personally make trading decisions.

## 7. STOCK Act, Enforcement, and the 2021–2025 Reform Effort

### 7.1 The Law as It Stands

The **Stop Trading on Congressional Knowledge (STOCK) Act of 2012** was passed nearly unanimously in the wake of a *60 Minutes* report alleging that some members of Congress had traded on non-public information. Key provisions:

- Affirms that members of Congress are subject to insider-trading laws.
- Requires public PTR filings within **45 days** of any trade over $1,000 (subsequently reduced to 30 days for some types).
- Prohibits use of non-public information for personal gain.
- Sets a **$200 late-filing fine** per violation, with discretion for ethics committees to waive (kapitol.ai; Campaign Legal Center; OpenSecrets).

Reporting since 2021 documents widespread non-compliance with limited consequ
